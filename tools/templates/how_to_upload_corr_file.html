How to Use Correlation for Grouping Stocks




What is Stock Correlation?


Stock correlation is a statistic that measures the degree to which two stocks move in relation to each other. The correlation coefficient can be a value between +1 (perfect positive correlation) and -1 (perfect negative correlation).


Why is it Important in Investing?


The most important aspect of investing is Risk Management. In other words, how can you reduce the chances of your portfolio losing money? One aspect of risk in a portfolio is correlation - if your portfolio is very correlated, it is likely taking on more risk than you think.

In this case, correlation is used to measure which stocks are likely to move up and together and can therefore be treated as similar assets in a portfolio. Correlation is a measure of risk since it determines the likelihood of your stocks increasing or decreasing together.

Therefore, if you reduce the correlation in your portfolio, you reduce the downside of your portfolio because the stocks you own are less likely to move up or down together in a given time period.

For example, most technology stocks (Apple, Amazon, Microsoft, Facebook etc) are highly correlated. Lets say Apple dives 10% this week - the odds are pretty high that the other technology stocks will drop about 10% too. So, to prevent your portfolio taking a 10% dive in one week, having stocks that aren't in the technology sector (aka aren't correlated), for example Ford Motor Company and Bank of America, the drop in your portfolio is likely to be far less severe.

You will notice that the table forms unique groupings in a diagonal pattern, where higher correlations tend towards yellow and negative correlation tends towards blue where high correlations cluster around a diagonal set of 1s. Each group should form a N x N matrix, indicating which stocks can be grouped together as well as a less correlated area, separating each group from the next.

You will often find that stocks in similar sectors or industries are grouped together - in this example, Apple (AAPL), Amazon (AMZN) and Facebook (FB) are all technology stocks and therefore highly correlated. Whereas Boeing (BAA) and Airbus (AIR) are both in the Aerospace and Defense industry, and are highly correlated with each other, but not with the technology stocks. Therefore, Group 1 can actually be labeled 'Technology' and Group 2 can be labeled 'Aerospace & Defense'.

Important Notes on the File that you Submit


  • The length of the period for which you want to perform correlation grouping does not matter. It can be the daily, weekly, monthly or even yearly closing prices for each stock.
  • Make sure the time period and time frame for each stock is the same.
  • The dates for each stock's prices must align. The model will not output the correct correlations if Apple stock's February data is in the March row. Apple's March data must go with Amazon's March data and both of theirs must go with Walmart's March data etc.
  • Ensure that you have a good amount of data. 3 monthly periods is too little to calculate an effective correlation. A good rule of thumb is to use a minimum of 25 periods.
  • It is important to note that some stocks may remain highly correlated over a long time frame, but this correlation may recede over time depending on various market factors and individual company performance.
